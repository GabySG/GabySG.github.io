Overview

What is the problem being addressed?

The information directly pertains to speed dating environments, but could be extrapolated to dating or attraction in general.

The “problems” addressed largely revolve around understanding what people look for most in a dating partner (e.g., personality qualities, physical attractiveness, demographic characteristics).  Variations around this central theme include: personal preferences, perceptions of the same gender’s preferences, perceptions of the opposite gender’s preferences, and perceptions of oneself compared to these preferences.


Where is the data coming from and what are its characteristics?

This dataset was originally collected as part of a speed dating study conducted by Raymond Fisman, Sheena Iyengar, Emir Kamenica, and Itamar Simonson.  The study methods are briefly summarized below.

The researchers ran 17 speed dating events from 2002-2004.  All of the speed dating participants were graduate and professional students at Columbia University.  Each session had between 20-40 participants, with a roughly even split of males and females.

Events were conducted at a popular bar/restaurant near the campus.  Participants sat in long rows of 2-person tables, with males on one side and females on the other.  Males rotated down the line so that by the end of the event they met all of the females.  Each rotation consisted of a 4-minute conversation and a 1-minute scorecard.  The scorecard included  whether the participant would want to see the other person again (yes/no), as well as six attributes that the participant rated their partner on.  All participants also completed questionnaires before and after the event.


We are interested in the following attributes/characteristics of the data.

  1. Demographics
    • Age
    • Gender
    • Race
    • Hometown
    • Field of Study
    • School attended for undergraduage degree
    • Intended career

  2. Event details
    • Numbers pf participants
    • Number of partners met

  3. Scoreboards
    • Match/mismatch for each pair(match=yes-yes, mismatch=yes-no, no-yes, no-no)
    • Ratings for each partner(100 points to distribute across 6 attributes)
      • Attractive
      • Sincere
      • Intelligent
      • Fun
      • Ambitious
      • Shared interests
    • Overall, how much do you like this person?
    • How probable do you think it is that this person willl say "yes" to you?
    • Have you met this person before?
    • How many matches do you estimate you will get?

  4. Pre-event survey questions
    • 100 points to distribute across 6 attributes
      • What do you look for in the opposite sex?
      • What do you think most of your fellow men/women look for in the opposite sex?
      • What do you think the opposite sex looks for in a date
      • How do you think you measure up?
      • How do you think others perceive you?
    • Importance of dating someone of the same racial/ethnic background
    • Importance of dating something of the same religious background
    • Hobbies
    • Many more…


Who would be interested in understanding more about this data?

Given that dating is such a broadly shared experience, we believe that almost anyone in the general public would be interested in these data.  Single people (obviously) and younger people may be especially interested, as these demographics are the most common participants of speed dating events.  Furthermore, certain researchers and academics may also be interested in these data if it pertains to their field of study (e.g., attraction, mate selection, etc.)


What questions would these people want to ask, and how would we vis help them answer those questions?

  • What are the rates of matches/mismatches?
    • How do men/women differ in what makes them say “Yes”?

  • What are the attributes/preferences/characteristics for the four (Yes, No) response combinations between two people that dated?
    • Do people prefer partners of the same demographic?
    • Are certain attributes more important than others?

  • Who was the ‘hottest’ person?
  • How do self-perceptions compare to other’s perceptions of you?  And does that differ if the other person voted yes vs. no?
  • How often do people go out on dates? Does it vary with age?
