Namaslay

Help Headphone users to choose the one their like
CHECK Namaslay

Overview

Objective

Headphones are now used to listen to music and podcasts or you can use them to speak to others during a call. They are used while working out, gaming, walking, or just lounging around. However, choosing a pair of suitable headphones sometimes are exhausting.

Our project is to help headphone users to

o Help them to quickly spot the headphone they need & want
o Decrease the time they were looking for the ideal one in a physical store


My role

I participated throughout the research process of contextual inquiry, interviews and survey. I took charge of the contextual inquiry, affinity diagram, and skeching journey maps and personas by collecting the findings. I also came up with two main design ideas which ended up as our final solution. Besides, I created Lo-fi design via Balsamiq and Hi-fi designs via Sketch.


Approach

The team started with two kinds of online websites (with all brands and websites with one specific brand) for headphones information. Then we conducted in-person observations and semi-structured interviews to have participants show/tell us how they shopped so that we may understand their purchase behavior.

Then we did contextual inquiries, and distributed a survey to identify problems that they face during purchasing in a physical store. Based on the data gathered, we did affinity diagram to categorize collected data. We used findings from the affinity diagram to develop user journey maps and personas.

These processes helped us understand our users better, and we identified problems in their headphone-purchasing experience. Then we organized divergent brainstorming design sessions with divergent design ideas. After doing a thorough analysis of their feasibility, creativity and to the biggest extent they can solve main problems, we then converged to # design ideas.

The ideas were presented to our class peers for feedback and evaluation by storyboards, wireframes. After multiple iterations, we came up with a mobile application called Nama-slay with a detailed user flow.

Know the users


Research

We initially conducted observations to observe their purchase process. They we did semi-structured interviews with users to find out the reasons behind their purchase, where they have looked for and what problems they have faced, and with salesperson who can share a summary of their interactions with users and best-selling products. After the first two steps, we distributed a survey with close-end questions which we are sure about the problem scope and open-end questions, which might bring us more surprises.

Then we decided to have an in-depth understanding of purchasing experience by doing contextual inquiry. After combining results of first-round survey and contextual inquiry, we did a second-round survey, which focused more on their thinking of problems what we extracted.


Observation

We conducted several in-person observations and semi-structured interviews at many local stores such as Best Buy, Apple, Target, and Barnes and Noble. We chose those research methods, since our objective was to have participants show/tell us how they shopped so that we may understand their purchase behaviour. We did not want to go with a structured interview or a survey initially, since we did not have sufficient information yet, to delve deeper into the problems buyers face currently.






At the Barnes and Noble we visited, there were two separate locations within the electronics section where headphones were displayed. One location had colorful packages of various brands of earbuds such as Skullcandy and Gumy. All of the headphones in this section had price tags. Items in this section were arranged by price, generally the cheapest ($9.98-$30) ones were the most accessible, followed the next price bracket (>$40) being in the locked lower section of the rack. The most expensive headphones (>$100) were kept in the top section. The other section was a small counter where speakers and headphones were set up for the purpose of letting customers try them on. There were no prices listed in this section. We noticed after multiple visits that these headphones were changed on occasion, though we are uncertain of the interval at which these products are swapped out.

Interview



We conducted semi-structured interviews, which turned out to be very helpful when we gather in-depth understanding of customers and sellers.


Contextual Inquiry

 

We did contextual inquiry both in BestBuy and Barnes and Noble, where we mainly interviewed three categories of users:

1. Young men who want to wear heaphones when playing games.

2. Young girls who want to wear heaphones when exercise.

3. Middle-age people who want to wear heaphones when travelling.




Affinity Diagram






Findings


  • I want to make an informed decision

    • I want to try the physical touch before making decision
    • I want to try the audio quality before making decision
    • I want more information provided in a store
    • I need more online information

  • I am looking for specific features
    • I want noise cancelling
    • I want to test my own music in a store
    • I only want to check several types of headphones I intend to buy

  • I have preference of certain price range for headphones
    • I have a prior budget
    • I want to save as much as I can

  • I am an experienced headphone user
    • I think I know a lot about headphone
    • I found some information provided are not necessary

  • Miscellaneous
    • I am willing to pay more for more features
    • I think the security gets in my way of seeing information
    • I go to a physical store because of geographic advantage
    • I have different headphones for different uses

Design Concepts


Our analysis gave us 3 major design ideas to work with: Kiosk to provide more information, a LED on-rack sorting system, and Personal assistant.



  • Kiosk showing more infromation related to headphones in store.
    •



    In this design, one kiosk will be exposed near the headphone section, users can get recommendations based on their choices on Use, Style and Features. In the recommendation page, users can click at the one he is interested in and see reviews of this headphone.

    Working Flow

    Choose Use, Style and Feature -> Get recommendation -> See reviews -> Price match




  • A LED on-rack sorting system
    •



    In this model, after users inputing their requirements, LEDs under headphones that meet requirements will show up.

    Working Flow

    Choose Use, Style and Feature -> Get shining LEDs under those headphones who meeet the requirements -> Users go check the recommended headphones



  • Personal assistant that customers can use their own cellphone to get information.
    •



    Customers can get use NFC to get the recommended headphone and be shown a real-time navigation map of a chosen headphone.

    Working Flow

    Choose brand/model type number -> Show similar products and their reviews separately -> Be guided to the headphone with a real-time store map




    User evaluation


    In this step, we showed three different sketches and working flow to experts, during when they were asked how they think of the sketch works.In order to avoid ordering bias during these studies, we switched the order on our designs up between the studies. We had

    A: the kiosk design, B: The LED design, and C: the personal assistant app design.
    We switched the order of presentation between ABC, CAB, BCA, etc for various users.

    We started with a quick introduction for the participants, explaining that they were looking at concepts for various systems that may improve the headphone shopping experience. We made sure to inform them that any comments, even negative ones, would be useful for improving and refining these designs. After the introduction, we took the users through the designs one by one and asked them a series of questions.

    Show them the pages for each design

    “Please look at the first screen for this design. Take a minute to look over it all and try to think aloud as you do so.
    If you have any questions, feel free to ask about them”.

    (if we’re asked “what does this do?”, ask them what they think it does before we give them an answer. Getting people to think about these things further will give us insight into what people think something is before us just giving the answer to them)

    By doing this, we got the users basic impressions of each screen. The thinking aloud made them more likely to vocalise interest, frustration, and confusion about various features. When the researcher needed further context about what they were reacting to, the researcher would ask.

    Explain to them the purpose of this page, and ask follow up questions now that this was to reset the user if they were altogether confused about that particular screen.


    "Is there any part of this screen that you particularly like or dislike?"

     The above question got at what were the important features to that user, and which ones were not necessary. This was useful in finding out what to add, as well as what things we as a team liked, but were useless to the average user.


    "Is there any feature that you would like added to this screen? If so, what is that feature?"

    By asking this question, we could find out if we had missed any important features in our design process. One such feature ended up being back buttons. We repeat these steps for the rest of the pages of that design.

    Ask about the design as a whole

     I. After getting design info about each part of a sketch, we then wanted them to see everything in context.


    “On a scale from 1-5, with 1 being not at all useful and 5 being extremely useful, how useful do you think this design would be?”
    “Knowing the whole context of the design, are there any features that stand out to you? Is this in a positive or negative way?”
    "Please share any other thoughts you have about this design, or any of the features involved."

     It’s always good to ask a user for their own thoughts on a design, as they may think of something that you didn’t.

    II. After having shown them all 3 designs


    “Which of these designs would you be most likely to use in a store?”

     This question was a direct query for one of our objectives. By asking the user which one they would be most likely to use, it would force them to think more about their actual use of the designs, and not just which one they liked the most.

    After finishing the feedback sessions for the sketches, we proceeded to design a wireframe for the Kiosk.

    Reasons supporting this decision are:

  • People like the way that they can get recommendations of headphone by inputing their requirements in use, style and feature.
  • People don't feel like using their own cellphone connected to a physical store in terms of security.
  • People find the lED annoying if they light up everytime a customer changes the requirements.
  • People like the navigation idea.
  • People love the icons.

    • So we consider these reviews when making the Lo-Fi prototype of the kiosk by combining things people like in three designs separately.
