Namaslay

Helping headphone users choose the headphones they won’t regret

  Objective

Headphones are now used while working out, gaming, walking, or just lounging around. However, choosing a pair of suitable headphones is exhausting.

Namaslay helps headphone users to quickly get recommendation of the headphone they need & want and decrease the time they were looking for the ideal one in a physical store.

  My Role

I participated throughout the research process of contextual inquiry, interviews and survey. I took charge of the contextual inquiry, affinity diagram, and skeching journey maps and personas by collecting the findings. I also came up with two main design ideas which ended up as our final solution. Besides, I created Lo-fi design via Balsamiq and Hi-fi designs via Sketch.

  Time&Team

NamaSlay was done between September to December of 2017 with my awesome teammates: Dillon, Ben, Akhil, Darren and Sally.

  Survey

The strength of conducting survey as a research method in our case is that it is a quick and efficient way for us to gather volumes of data within the structured framework of hypothesis that we intentionally designed. It’s low-cost, easy to complete, timely, and allows for uniform question presentation, and allows for anonymity and confidentiality.


We distributed the 1st survey with 82 responses to probe into headphone users' likes and dislikes when they choose a set of headphones.

Key takeaways from the first survey are 70% of users in research reported that they prefer to purchase headphones online and most of people people wear different types of headphones in different situations.

We did a 2nd-round survey with 172 valid responses, we focused more on their in-store purchase experience, E.g, what they think are missing in store to prevent them from making decision.


More than after of people choose to not buying or buying online even after they went to store. We took this to mean there is something lacking in physical store that prevents people from making decisions. In this case We see a lot of opportunity to improve the physical store purchasing experience.

Therefore, We narrow down our scope to the users in physical stores who tend to do less research prior in-store purchase, specifically we will be studying the Georgia Tech Barnes and Noble due to its accessibility on campus after getting permission from the manager.

  Interview

We conducted semi-structured interviews with salespeople in local stores. We asked them about how many sets of headphones can be sold and what kind of difficulties do people come across and ask them for help?

Their responses helped us to confirm our findings from surveys: people always have specific requirements before coming to physical stores.

  Contextual Inquiry



We did contextual inquiry both in BestBuy and Barnes and Noble, where we found distinct and typical different requirements from three categories of users:

1. Young men who want to wear heaphones when playing games.
2. Middle-age people who want to wear heaphones when travelling.
3. Young girls who want to wear heaphones when exercise.


Define

  Affinity Diagram

By collecting data from interviews, contextual inquiries and surveys, we began to do affinity mapping to categorize our findings.

1. I want to make an informed decision

  • I want to try the physical touch before making decision
  • I want to try the audio quality before making decision
  • I want more information provided in a store
  • I need more online information

2. I am looking for specific features
  • I want noise cancelling
  • I want to test my own music in a store
  • I only want to check several types of headphones I intend to buy

3. I have preference of certain price range for headphones
  • I have a prior budget
  • I want to save as much as I can

4. I am an experienced headphone user
  • I think I know a lot about headphone
  • I found some information provided are not necessary

5. Miscellaneous

  • I'm willing to pay more for more features
  • I think the security gets in my way of seeing information
  • I go to a physical store because of geographic advantage
  • I have different headphones for different uses




  User Requirement

Instead of trying to solve every problem, we decided to put more efforts in solving the most important and urgent ones.



It turned out that 3 of them are the most important and have been waitting to be solved urgently: 1. Buyers come to the store with specific requirements and want their headphones to meet the requirments 2. People love to see what previous customers think about it, a.k.a customer reviews and 3. People, epecially the young who explore a lot & without high affordability, want to pay the lowest price.

As far as 4. People trying out headphones can help them to make the decision, we are informed that display of try-out racks are handled by the vendors themselves after communicating with managers in BestBuy and Barnes & Noble, rather than the physical store. So considering the feasibility, we compromised to a navigation part to guide people to try-out racks instead of changing the try-out rack itself.

Ideate

  Brainstorming

Our analysis gave us 3 major design ideas to work with: Kiosk to provide more information, LED on-rack sorting system, and Personal assistant.

In this design, one kiosk will be exposed near headphone rack, users get recommendations based on their choices on Use, Style and Features. In the recommendation page, users can click at the one he is interested in and see reviews of this headphone.

Working Flow:

Choose favored Use, Style & Feature -> Get recommendation -> See reviews/Price match



In this model, after users inputing their requirements, LEDs under headphones that meet requirements will light up.

Working Flow:

Choose Use, Style and Feature -> Get shining LEDs under those headphones who meet the requirements -> Users go check the lighted headphones

Customers can use NFC tech (Near-field communication) to get information of the pair of headphone they are looking for and get a real-time navigation map of a chosen headphone.

Working Flow:

Choose brand/model type number -> Show products and their reviews separately -> Be guided to the headphone with a real-time store map

  User evaluation

In this step, we showed all of three different sketches and working flow to users, during when they were asked how they think the sketched idea works. In order to avoid order-bias during the study, We had

A: Kiosk   B: LED   and C: Personal assistant
We switched the order of presentation between ABC, CAB, BCA, etc for various users.


We started with a quick introduction for the participants, explaining that they were looking at concepts for various systems that may improve the headphone shopping experience. We made sure to inform them that any comments, even negative ones, would be useful for improving and refining these designs.

After the introduction, we took the users through the designs one by one and asked them a series of questions.

1. Show them the pages for each design

“Please look at the first screen for this design. Take a minute to look over it all and try to think aloud as you do so.
If you have any questions, feel free to ask about them”.
By doing this, we got the users basic impressions of each screen. The thinking aloud made them more likely to vocalise interest, frustration, and confusion about various features. When the researcher needed further context about what they were reacting to, the researcher would ask.

Explain to them the purpose of this page, and ask follow up questions now that this was to reset the user if they were altogether confused about that particular screen.

"Is there any part of this screen that you particularly like or dislike?"
 The above question got at what were the important features to that user, and which ones were not necessary. This was useful in finding out what to add, as well as what things we as a team liked, but were useless to the average user.

"Is there any feature that you would like added to this screen? If so, what is that feature?"
 By asking this question, we could find out if we had missed any important features in our design process. One such feature ended up being back buttons. We repeat these steps for the rest of the pages of that design.

2. Ask about the design as a whole

I. After getting design info about each part of a sketch, we then wanted them to see everything in context.

“On a scale from 1-5, with 1 being not at all useful and 5 being extremely useful, how useful do you think this design would be?”
“Knowing the whole context of the design, are there any features that stand out to you? Is this in a positive or negative way?”
"Please share any other thoughts you have about this design, or any of the features involved."
 It’s always good to ask a user for their own thoughts on a design, as they may think of something that you didn’t.

II. After having shown them all 3 designs

“Which of these designs would you be most likely to use in a store?”
 This question was a direct query for one of our objectives. By asking the user which one they would be most likely to use, it would force them to think more about their actual use of the designs, and not just which one they liked the most.
